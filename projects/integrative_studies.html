The Biotic and Environmental History of Amazonia:
Integrative Studies

Understanding how a large biota and environmental system such as Amazonia was assembled over time calls for an integrative perspective. Our Dimensions of Biodiversity project is designed to see the evolution of the Amazonian biota and its large-scale landscape as an integrated whole. Knowing how tectonics and paleogeography are causally linked to each other and to landscape change and all those, in turn, to biotic change is essential. Thus, paleogeographic hypotheses place constraints on reconstructions of biotic evolution (such as when geological evidence points to the presence of wetlands or terra firme forest in a region). And inferences about biotic history can be used to postulate or test paleogeographic scenarios (e.g., organismal time trees enable us to postulate time constraints on geological events, such as the temporal formation of rivers). In the end, we seek a reconciliation of Earth and biotic history.

The same goes for understanding how paleoclimatic changes have had an influence on regional patterns of community stability and change. We can integrate remote sensing, environmental and climate modeling, and palynology with historical biogeography, phylogeography, population genetics, and landscape ecology to begin answering these questions. Our Team has already begun to undertake various integrative studies (see Ribas et al. 2012. Proc. Roy. Soc. 279B:681-689), and more will follow in the near future.


The accompanying figure summarizes the many disciplines involved in our Project, all designed to understand the general question of the history of Amazonia. All of them contribute a piece to solving that puzzle. And as noted above, there are a host of other questions that can be investigated by research that integrates among disciplines.
