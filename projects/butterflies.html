Butterfly Research

For more that 200 years, the butterflies of the Amazon basin have intrigued naturalists because of their brightly-colored, geographically variable wing patterns. When Henry Walter Bates took his historic trip up the Amazon in the mid-19th century, one of his most important discoveries related to the patterns and relationships of mimetic butterflies. Subsequently, scientists have devoted decades to documenting the distributions and relationships of these species and their geographical variants.

The goal of our part of the Dimensions project is to assess these patterns of variation using modern DNA sequencing technologies, to address the following questions:

  1. Are there congruent patterns of variation among different species that co-occur in the Amazon basin?
  2. If these congruent distributional patterns exist, do the relationships among them reflect similar patterns of evolutionary diversification over time?
  3. If there are common patterns of diversification (congruent phylogenetic trees), do molecular clocks suggest that the patterns were similar in time as well as in space.

This is a large interdisciplinary project, and even the butterfly component will rely upon the cooperative expertise of many lepidopterists who are working in complementary areas of research. We look forwards to developing these collaborations as the research proceeds.
