Documenting Biodiversity

Herbarium Specimen Georeferencing

For the past 250 years, botanists have documented the plants of a region by collecting portions of plants, flattening and drying them, and gluing them to stiff sheets of a paper, along with a label containing textual information such as the name of the plant, when and where it was collected, and by whom. These samples become permanent museum specimens and are stored in repositories called herbaria. There are about 2800 active herbaria in the world, containing about 335 million specimens. Brazil has approximately 125 active herbaria containing about 6,800,000 specimens. There are probably between 1 and 1-5 million herbarium specimens in from Amazonia, distributed among approximately 75 herbaria worldwide. The overwhelming majority of specimens have been made since 1900; specimens collected before that date originate from only about six different exploring expeditions. Probably the single largest collecting effort in the region was the Projeto Flora Amazonica project, 1977-1984, which resulted in about 33,000 collections.


For this project, we will assemble herbarium specimens of plants from throughout Amazonia focusing first on Bignoniaceae and Lecythidaceae, but including specimens from as many other groups as possible. Data from the relevant specimens have already been digitized, e.g., digital images have been captured of the specimens and the label data has been transcribed into an electronic database. In order to be able to plot on a map the location where each specimen was collected, we will need to have a numerical presentation of that site, i.e., latitude and longitude values. Because many specimens in the dataset for this project were collected before the time when collectors routinely carried geopositioning devices with them in the field, the geographic coordinates were not gathered at the time of collection, and must be added retroactively through a process called georeferencing. Georeferencing involves using a variety of tools, including Google Earth, gazetteers, collector’s field books and paper maps to add approximate the location for each collection.


Plant phylogeney and phylogeography

The Bignoniaceae are mainly pantropical with only a few representatives found in temperate zones. Tropical America, with ca. 80 genera and 600 species, is the center of diversity of the family. Molecular phylogenetic data support the monophylly of the family, excluding Paulownia (= Paulowniaceae) and Schlegelia (= Schlegeliaceae). The same studies suggest that Neotropical tribe Bignoniaceae is also monophyletic. This tribe is endemic to the Neotropics, includes all the lianas and shrubs with capsules, and consists approximately 350 species in 50 genera; thus, the plant groups studies with include three life forms: trees (Lecythidaceae) and shrubs and lianas (Bignoniaceae).


Members of the Bignoniaceae exhibit various patterns of flowering phenology which, together these are hypothesized to allow the maintenance of high species diversity by allowing different species to share limited pollinator resources. The spectacular flowers of Bignoniaceae are mostly pollinated by bees and wasps but butterflies, hawkmoths, and bats are also pollinators. Most of the species of this group are dry capsules with winged seeds that are either wind- or water-dispersed but a few species have seeds that are ornamented, a feature that facilitates adherence and dispersal by animals.
